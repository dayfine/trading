User-endorsed framing (2026-06-11, from another conversation) for why cap-weighted
SPX is structurally hard to beat — adopted as the program's evaluation lens:

1. **Sharpe's arithmetic**: average active dollar = index minus costs; beating it
   is zero-sum vs professionals with a 1-2%/yr cost headwind (SPIVA: ~88-90% of
   large-cap active underperforms over 15y).
2. **Bessembinder skewness**: ~4% of companies create essentially all net wealth;
   most stocks underperform T-bills lifetime. Cap-weighting uniquely holds every
   mega-winner at full weight forever and never sells; ANY winner-trimming rule
   amputates the right tail that is the entire return. This is the outside-in
   derivation of [[project_edge_is_the_fat_tail]] — our six WF-CV-rejected
   winner-touching mechanisms are instances of a law, not implementation bugs.
3. **Self-cleaning + frictions**: losers shrink themselves out at zero cost;
   3-4% turnover, 2-3bp fees. (Flip side: index never exits — rides every −50%.)

**Implications adopted:**
- The rolling-start matrix result (median ≈+1pp TR-adjusted vs SPX on 2011-2026,
  [[project-rolling-start-matrix-first-run]]) is SPIVA reproduced in-house — a
  VALIDATION of the measurement lens, not a surprising failure.
- Weinstein stage analysis is **definitionally winner-touching** (Stage-3/4 exits
  sell what the index rides). Expect it to LOSE the bull-window CAGR race; its
  economic case is bear-regime exit (never ride Stage 4) → the decisive tests are
  bear-containing windows (2000-2011 matrix) + capital-DD / time-underwater /
  start-robustness / barbell Calmar ([[project_barbell_on_stocks]],
  [[project_evaluation_methodology_reframe]]) — NOT raw CAGR vs SPX in bulls.
- "Beats SPX" claims must survive: out-of-sample (WF-CV+DSR — have), realistic
  costs (cost-model — have), and not-disguised-factor-loading (do NOT have — a
  factor/beta decomposition lens is a named gap; candidate future work).
